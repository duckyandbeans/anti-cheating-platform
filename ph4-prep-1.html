1. Common Mistakes: Singular vs. Plural

The Golden Rule of Subject-Verb Agreement

A singular subject takes a singular verb, and a plural subject takes a plural verb. While this sounds simple, it gets tricky when words come between the subject and the verb, or when dealing with indefinite pronouns.

  • Prepositional Phrases: The subject is never in a prepositional phrase. (The list of items is on the desk.)
  • Indefinite Pronouns: Words like everybody, anyone, nobody, each, and someone are always singular. (Everybody knows the answer.)
  • Or / Nor: When subjects are joined by or or nor, the verb agrees with the subject closest to it. (Either the teacher or the students are late.)

2. Common Mistakes: Word Formation

Using the Correct Parts of Speech

A frequent error is confusing nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. Paying attention to suffixes (word endings) can help you identify and use the correct form.

  • Nouns (Things/Concepts): Often end in -tion, -ment, -ness, -ity, -ance/ence. (e.g., Development)
  • Verbs (Actions): Often end in -ize, -ate, -en, -ify. (e.g., Memorize)
  • Adjectives (Describing nouns): Often end in -al, -ful, -ous, -ive, -able. (e.g., Successful)
  • Adverbs (Describing verbs/adjectives): Usually end in -ly. (e.g., Carefully)

3. Impersonal Language & Passive Voice

Distancing the Writer

Academic and formal writing often requires an objective tone. We achieve this by using impersonal structures or the passive voice to shift focus from the 'doer' to the action itself.

  • The "It" Pattern: It + passive verb + that + clause. (It is said that...)
  • The Subjective Pattern: Subject + passive verb + to-infinitive. (The children are said to be...)

4. Reported Speech

The Rules of Backshifting

When reporting what someone else said, we typically shift the tense back one step in time (e.g., Present Simple becomes Past Simple). You also need to adjust pronouns and specific time/place expressions so the sentence makes sense from the reporter's perspective.
